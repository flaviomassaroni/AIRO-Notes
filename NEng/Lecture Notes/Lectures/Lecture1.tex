\subsection{Historical Background}

The \textbf{Electroencephalogram} (EEG) is the non-invasive detection of electrical signals from the scalp generated by cortical neurons.

The alpha rhythm is a spontaneous EEG oscillation typically ranging between 8 and 13 Hz, 
first identified by Hans Berger in the 1920s. Historically, based on the observations of Lord Adrian and Matthews,
it was considered an "idling" state of the brain. Because alpha synchronization is most prominent over the occipital 
cortex during relaxed, eyes-closed states and desynchronizes upon opening the eyes or engaging in visual tasks, 
it was originally thought to merely represent functional inactivity.

However, modern research challenges the strict idling hypothesis, 
revealing that alpha oscillations also play an active role in cognitive processing. 
Studies have shown that alpha rhythms can emerge even with open eyes during visual working memory tasks.

\subsection{The brain}

The brain can be modeled as a complex, non-linear, time-variant dynamical system, where the waves recorded on the scalp represent the macroscopic output of neural activity. 
Because these spontaneous oscillations vary between individual trials even for the same stimulus, it is standard practice to average multiple recordings to extract consistent, time-locked signals, such as \textbf{Event-Related Potentials} (ERPs).
An ERP is a change of electrical potential registered immediately after a given input to the brain, that are also spontaneous rythms that
do not need a time defined event. 
The averaging procedure is a key concept to be capable of distinguish between the ERPs and the electrical noise (called "Artifact").


To understand the amplitude of these macroscopic EEG waves, it is crucial to introduce the concept of \textbf{Synchronization}. 
A brain rhythm is a neurophysiological phenomenon that directly reflects the coordinated firing of neuronal populations.
The amplitude of the EEG signal is strictly tied to this coordination: neural synchronization results in an amplitude increase, whereas desynchronization leads to an amplitude decrease. 

A classic example is the alpha rhythm, which synchronizes (increases in amplitude) when a subject is resting with eyes closed, 
and desynchronizes (decreases in amplitude) upon opening the eyes.

This principle also extends to ERPs: early, 
low-latency components reflect highly synchronized activity within primary cortices, whereas later components involve more desynchronized, 
widely distributed neural networks.

It is crucial to understand the sensibility and the precision of this tool. Indeed, the order of the signal is micro-Volts, around typically 5–50 µV, rarely up to 100 µV.

\subsection{Brain Rhythms}

Brain activity is characterized by oscillations at various frequencies, which are grouped into specific bands. Each rhythm is associated with particular brain regions and physiological or cognitive states.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Alpha Rhythm (8--13 Hz):} Traditionally considered an ``idling'' rhythm, it is most prominent over the occipital lobe (visual cortex). 
    Its amplitude is highly responsive to visual input: it increases (synchronizes) when the eyes are closed and decreases (desynchronizes) when the eyes are open. 
    The amplitude typically waxes and wanes over seconds.
    
    \item \textbf{Mu Rhythm (8--13 Hz):} This is the motor-cortex counterpart of the alpha rhythm. It is located over the central (sensorimotor) cortex and is 
    primarily visible on central electrodes (often the blue channels in topographic maps). It frequently exhibits a ``comb-shaped'' waveform with asymmetric peaks.
    
    \item \textbf{Beta Rhythm (14--30 Hz):} Associated with active motor and cognitive processing. It is observed across multiple cortical areas. 
    The typical band is 15--30 Hz, though it is often cited around 20--25 Hz.
    
    \item \textbf{Theta Rhythm (4--7.5 Hz):} Linked to memory, workload, and stress. It is prominent in midline structures, such as the hippocampus and frontal 
    midline, and its amplitude increases during demanding cognitive tasks.
    
    \item \textbf{Delta Rhythm ($<$ 3.5 Hz):} Characterized by very slow waves (typically $<$ 3.3 Hz). In healthy adults, these are rare during wakefulness and 
    usually indicate deep sleep or pathological states.

\end{itemize}
