\section[Lecture 2 -- {\it Kinematic Redundancy}]{Kinematic Redundancy}

\subsection{Introduction}
Defining the direct Kinematics of the task $y=k(q)$, which is the map between the task space and the configuration space
$$
k:\mathit{C} \rightarrow \mathit{T}
$$
where dim$(\mathit{C}) = n$ and dim$(\mathit{T}) = m$, a robot is said to be \textbf{Kinematically redundant} for the task if $n>m$ (i.e. there are more degrees 
of fredom than strictly needed for executing the task). The redundancy concept is relative to a given task.
A redundant robot have an \textit{infinite number of solution} for the inverse kinematics in the regular case, and this redundancy can be 
used in many ways, such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item to avoid collision with obstacles in Cartesian space;
    \item to avoid kinematic singularities in Joint space;
    \item to stay within the admissible joint ranges;
    \item to increase manipulability in specified directions;
    \item to uniformly distribute/limit joint velocities and/or accelerations;
    \item to optimize energy consumption or needed motion torques;
    \item to minimize execution time on a given Carthesian path;
    \item to increase dependability with respecto to actuation faults
    \item \dots
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deepening the math behind kinematic redundancy}

For an extensive comprehension of the topic, let us consider the math behind the Kinematic redundancy.
The jacobian matrix $J$ maps the joints' velocities $\dot{q} \in \R^n$ to the task velocities $\dot{y}\in \R^n$ as it follows:
$$
\dot{y}=J\dot{q}
$$
In a redundant situation, since $ m < n $, the jacobian matrix $J$ is a non squared matrix $m \times n$, thus the system is underdetermined and there exists infinite solutions
for $\dot{q}$ for a same $\dot{y}$. The \textbf{Null Space} is the set of all the joints' velocities which produces $\dot{y} = 0$
$$
N(J) = \{\dot{q} : J\dot{q}=0\}
$$

Since m < n, $dim(N(J)) = m-n>0$, and it is said a \textbf{Non-trivial space}, which means it cannot be related to an all zeros-vector, thus it has linearly independent columns.

We can generated the \textbf{Projection Matrix P}, an $n \times n$ matrix whom image (range) concides with $N(J)$, thus ${R}(P) \equiv N(J)$. 
It means that every vector $P\dot{q}_N$, $\dot{q}_N \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is in the null space of J.
$$
J \cdot P = O \implies J(P\dot{q}_N) = 0 \quad \forall \dot{q}_N$$

This mathematical statement illustrates why the projection matrix is crucial:
it maps any generalized velocity vector into the null space of the Jacobian, ensuring that the resulting 
motion does not affect the end-effector's primary task.

The general solution for the joint velocities $\dot{q}$ that produce a desired task velocity $\dot{y}^*$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
    \dot{q} = \dot{q}^* + P \dot{q}_N
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\dot{q}^*$ is a particular solution (e.g., the minimum-norm solution $\dot{q}^* = J^{\#} \dot{y}^*$).
    \item $P \dot{q}_N$ is the orthogonal projection into the null space of the Jacobian, with $\dot{q}_N$ being an arbitrary joint velocity vector.
\end{itemize}

It has a strong physical meaning. The term $P\dot{q}_N$ generates \textbf{self-motions} or \textbf{internal motions}, which consists in the robot moving and changing the overall configuration, 
yet the end-effector remains perfectly stationary in task space. And this is possible thanks to Kinematic Redundancy.
From a control perspective, this property enables a fundamental \textit{decoupling between the primary task and the interal configuration} of the robot. While the particual
solution $\dot{q}^*$ is responsible for executing the desired task velocity $\dot{y}^*$, the null space component $P\dot{q}_N$ can be chosen freely to pursue a \textit{secondary objective}
which won't interfer with the primary task. A particular and common choice regards a manipulability measure $w(q)$, which steers the robot away from singular configurations.
\begin{equation}
    \dot{q}_N = k \nabla w(q)
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{equation}
    w(q) = \sqrt{\det(J J^T)}
\end{equation}
and $k > 0$ is a scalar gain.

$\dot{q}_N$ can be also be chosen as a gradient-based penalty on joint proximity 
to physical limits, which keeps each joint away from its mechanical stops and preserves future maneuverability. Or even as a and obstacle-aware terms that push
 links away from detected objects in the environment, allowing the robot to reconfigure its elbow or intermediate links around an obstacle 
 without perturbing the gripper trajectory at all.

The \textbf{general solution} is always a particular solution plus a null space component, expressed as:

\begin{equation}
    \dot{q} = \dot{q}^* + P \dot{q}_N \label{eq:IKProbSol}
\end{equation}

which represents the foundations of kinematic redundancy.
This rests on a rigorous theoretical foundation rooted in linear algebra, since linear ($\dot{y} = J\dot{q}$ is a system of linear equations). And it is also shown
that since $JP =0$, 
\begin{equation}
J\dot{q} = J(\dot{q}^* + P\dot{q}_N) = J\dot{q}^* + JP\dot{q}_N = \dot{y}^* + 0 = \dot{y}^*    
\end{equation}

and $P\dot{q}_N$ does not disturb the movement of the principal task.
From an optimization standpoint, this structure provides a clean separation of concerns: 
$\dot{q}^*$ is entirely responsible for satisfying the primary task, while the remaining degrees of freedom, captured by the free vector $\dot{q}_N$,
are available to optimize a secondary objective.

\subsection{Inverse kinematics problem}
The inverse kinematics problem for a redundant robot consists of finding $\mathbf{q}(t)$ such that $\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{q}(t)) = \mathbf{y}(t)$ for all times $t$. 
Since the robot has more degrees of freedom than strictly needed for the task, infinitely many solutions exist---even when the task vector is kept constant, 
$\mathbf{y}(t) = \bar{\mathbf{y}}$. The extra degrees of freedom give rise to joint displacements that are unobservable at the task level, 
in the sense that they do not contribute to the end-effector motion: these are precisely the self-motions introduced earlier.
Solutions to the inverse kinematics problem are mainly sought at the differential (velocity) level. By considering the Jacobian matrix, 
which provides a linear mapping between the tangent spaces of the configuration manifold $\mathcal{C}$ and the task manifold $\mathcal{T}$, the problem is greatly simplified.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \label{fig:TangentSpacesMapping}
\end{figure}

The torus $\mathcal{C} = S^1 \times S^1$ represents the configuration space of a 2R robot, where each joint varies in the interval $[-\pi, \pi]$ with periodic boundary conditions.
The plane $T_\mathbf{q}\mathcal{C}$ represents the tangent space at a given configuration $\mathbf{q}$, which contains the joint velocity vectors $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$.

On the right side, $\mathcal{T} = \mathbb{R}^2$ represents the task space (e.g., the position of the end-effector in the Cartesian plane), and $T_{\mathbf{y}}\mathcal{T}$ 
is its corresponding tangent space containing the task velocities $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ (or $\dot{\mathbf{y}}$).

The Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q})$ acts as a linear mapping between these tangent spaces, $T_\mathbf{q}\mathcal{C} \to T_{\mathbf{y}}\mathcal{T}$. 
Since the robot is non-redundant ($n = m = 2$), the inverse mapping is uniquely defined by $\mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{q})$, provided the robot is not in a singular configuration.

$n=2$ and $m=1$.

In this case we can solve the IK problem through the \textit{general redundancy resolution}, which is an optimization problem that can be solved offline (globally) or online (locally).
The objective funcion is a general function of the state space $(\mathbf{q}\dot{\mathbf{q}})$, and the goal is to find a trajectory $\mathbf{q}(t)$ and $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$.

Note: We are in the time domain, $\mathbf{q}(t)$ and $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$ are joints position and velocity with respect to t. $H$($\mathbf{q}(t)$ and $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$) is a \textbf{Goal Function},
(that we can consider as a Cost function): it evaluates how good is a configuration or a movement. 
The function $H$ can be seen in a more global way:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Minimizing the energy consumption}: $H(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}) = \frac{1}{2}\dot{\mathbf{q}}^T\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q})\dot{\mathbf{q}}$
    \item \textbf{To get away from singularities}: $H(\mathbf{q}) = \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{J} \mathbf{J}^T)}$
    \item \textbf{To avoid an Obstacle}: $H(\mathbf{q})$ can be the inverse of the distance between the robot arm and the obstacle;
\end{itemize}  

To better understand how this optimization is carried out, we introduce the two main methodologies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Global methods (Offline)}: This approach looks at the problem in its entirety. Given a desired task trajectory $\mathbf{y}(t)$ and an initial configuration $\mathbf{q}(t_0)$, 
    finding an evolution over the entire time window $[t_0, t_0+T]$ involves a functional of the type:
    \begin{equation}
        \int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} H(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}) dt   
    \end{equation}
    which has to be minimized over the whole trajectory $\mathbf{q}(t)$. 
    
    \textit{Why is it difficult?} The mathematics behind this relies on the Calculus of Variations and produces a Two-Point Boundary Value (TPBV) problem, 
    resulting in highly non-linear differential equations. Consequently, it cannot be computed in real-time while the robot is moving; the entire optimal trajectory must be pre-calculated 
    offline before execution.
    
    \item \textbf{Local methods (Online)}: This is the approach used in the vast majority of real-world robotic applications. Instead of optimizing over the entire journey, 
    the controller evaluates only the current time step $t = k T_s$ (where $T_s$ is the sampling time, e.g., $1$ ms). 
    The objective becomes much simpler: given the instantaneous task velocity $\dot{\mathbf{y}}$ required right now, what is the joint velocity $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$ that realizes the 
    task while simultaneously optimizing $H$ at this exact instant?

    \textit{Why is it easier?} Mathematically, this reduces to a Linear-Quadratic (LQ) optimization problem, which modern processors can solve instantaneously. Once the optimal joint 
    velocity $\dot{\mathbf{q}}(kT_s)$ is found, the joint positions are updated using Euler integration, see Appendix \ref{sec:euler_appendix}:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{q}((k+1)T_s) = \mathbf{q}(kT_s) + T_s \dot{\mathbf{q}}(kT_s)
    \end{equation}

where $k$ is an integer index ($k = 0, 1, 2, \dots$) representing the current control cycle. 

To better understand its physical meaning, we can break down the equation as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\mathbf{q}((k+1)T_s)$: The joint configuration that the robot must reach at the \textbf{next time step}.
        \item $\mathbf{q}(kT_s)$: The joint configuration at the \textbf{current time instant}.
        \item $T_s \dot{\mathbf{q}}(kT_s)$: The small (angular) \textbf{displacement} the joints will undergo. 
        This term is computed by taking the optimal velocity calculated right now ($\dot{\mathbf{q}}$) and multiplying it by the duration of the time interval 
        until the next update ($T_s$).
    \end{itemize}

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Local resolution methods}
Three are the classes of methods for solving $J(q)\dot{q}=\dot{y}$
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Jacobian-based methods}: Among the infinite solutions, the one choses in the one that mininimizes a suitable (possibly weighted) norm;
    \item \textbf{Null-space methods}: A term is added to the previous solution so as not to affect execution ot the task trajectory, i.e. that beloing to the null space $N(J(q))$
    \item \textbf{Task augmentation methods}: redundancy is reduced/eliminated by adding $s \leq n-m$ further auxiliary tasks (when $s = n-m$, the problem has been “squared”)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Jacobian-based methods}
These methods tackle the redundancy problem at a differential kinematics level:
\begin{equation}
    J(\mathbf{q})\dot{\mathbf{q}} = \dot{\mathbf{y}}
\end{equation}
where $J \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the Jacobian matrix with $n > m$. We seek local solutions in the linear form:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf{q}} = K\dot{\mathbf{y}}
\end{equation}
where $K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$. Substituting this into the differential equation yields $J(K\dot{\mathbf{y}}) = \dot{\mathbf{y}}$. For this to hold for any arbitrary desired task velocity $\dot{\mathbf{y}}$ in the range of $J$ ($\dot{\mathbf{y}} \in \mathcal{R}(J)$), the matrix $K$ must satisfy the condition:
\begin{equation}
    JKJ = J
\end{equation}
Such a matrix $K$ is called a \textbf{generalized inverse} of $J$ (which depends on the current configuration $\mathbf{q}$). Because the system is underdetermined ($n > m$), there are infinitely many matrices that satisfy this condition.

\paragraph{Right Inverse}
A basic example of a generalized inverse is the \textit{right inverse}. Let the Jacobian be partitioned as $J = \begin{pmatrix} J_a & J_b \end{pmatrix}$, where $J_a \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ is a square, non-singular submatrix ($\det J_a \neq 0$). The right inverse is constructed as:
\begin{equation}
    K_r = \begin{pmatrix} J_a^{-1} \\ \mathbf{0} \end{pmatrix} \implies \dot{\mathbf{q}} = \begin{pmatrix} \dot{\mathbf{q}}_a \\ \dot{\mathbf{q}}_b \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} J_a^{-1}\dot{\mathbf{y}} \\ \mathbf{0} \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Physically, this approach corresponds to ''freezing'' $n-m$ degrees of freedom ($\dot{\mathbf{q}}_b = \mathbf{0}$) and using only the remaining $m$ joints to execute the task. This solution is trivial and rarely optimal, as it does not exploit the redundancy of the manipulator.

\paragraph{Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse}
To actively exploit redundancy, we typically choose the \textbf{Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse}, denoted as $J^\#$ (see Appendix \ref{sec:Pseudoinverse}). 
When the Jacobian is full row rank ($\text{rank}(J) = m$), it is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    J^\# = J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}
\end{equation}
yielding the joint velocity command $\dot{\mathbf{q}}_{PS} = J^\#\dot{\mathbf{y}}$. 
Among all the infinite generalized inverses, the pseudoinverse is unique because it provides the \textbf{minimum norm solution} in a least-squares sense. 

Specifically, the pseudoinverse solution $\dot{\mathbf{q}}_{PS}$ solves the following hierarchical optimization problem:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\dot{\mathbf{q}}} \|\dot{\mathbf{q}}\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \dot{\mathbf{q}} \in \arg\min_{\dot{\mathbf{q}}} \|\dot{\mathbf{y}} - J\dot{\mathbf{q}}\|^2
\end{equation}
This means that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\dot{\mathbf{y}} \in \mathcal{R}(J)$, it finds the solution that satisfies $J\dot{\mathbf{q}} = \dot{\mathbf{y}}$ exactly while minimizing $\|\dot{\mathbf{q}}\|^2$.
    \item If $\dot{\mathbf{y}} \notin \mathcal{R}(J)$ (e.g., in a singularity), it first minimizes the task reconstruction error $\|\dot{\mathbf{y}} - J\dot{\mathbf{q}}\|^2$ and then, 
    among the best approximants, picks the one with minimum norm.
\end{itemize}

If $J$ is singular and $JJ^T$ cannot be inverted, the closed-form $J^\# = J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}$ is not applicable. 
In such cases, $J^\#$ must be computed through numerical methods such as the \textbf{Singular Value Decomposition} (SVD) of $J$ (see Appendix \ref{sec:SVD}).
\begin{equation}
    J = U \Sigma V^T
\end{equation}



The SVD has special properties. 

The matrix $\Sigma$ has the folowing form:
$$
    \Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & & & \\ 
        & \ddots & & 0_{m\times (n-m)}\\ 
        & & \sigma_m & 
    \end{pmatrix}
$$
and
$$ \sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\dots \ge \sigma_\rho>0 $$
$$ \sigma_{\rho+1}= \sigma_{\rho+2}\dots = \sigma_{m}=0 $$

where $\rho$ is the rank of $J$.
The numbers $\sigma_1\dots ,\sigma_\rho$ are the \textbf{singular values} of $J$. 
The eigen vectors of $JJ^T$ (columns of $U$) are associated to the eigen values $\sigma_1^2\dots,\sigma_\rho^2$.
The last $n-\rho$ columns of $V$ are a basis for the null space of $J$.

\paragraph{Handling Singularities}
The pseudoinverse approach fails when the robot falls into a kinematic singularity 
($\text{rank}(J) < m$), as the matrix $JJ^T$ becomes singular and non-invertible. 
Furthermore, in the close vicinity of a singularity, the joint velocities computed by $J^\#$ tend to infinity. 
To guarantee safe and bounded velocities, the exact tracking constraint is relaxed using the \textbf{Damped Least Squares (DLS)} method (Appendix \ref{sec:DLS}), 
which introduces a damping factor to balance tracking accuracy and feasible joint speeds.
